## The Empire That Should Have Died
Every treaty signed in Paris says the same thing. Austria-Hungary is finished. The Czechs leave. The Poles leave. The South Slavs leave. The maps are redrawn before the ink dries on the armistice.

It doesn't happen cleanly here.

A faction inside Vienna — linguists, officers, men who believed in the river more than any flag — refuses the terms. Not all of them. Not even most. A handful, in the right offices, holding the eastern marches through the first winter by the kind of stubborn bureaucratic inertia that moves faster than armies.

Ivan Baler is not in Vienna yet. He is in Zagreb, at a table, with a grammar he has been studying for three weeks straight. He is about to make a trip north.

They don't save the empire. They build something the empire never had the nerve to become.

### 1919 The Man Before the State
His name was Ivan Baler. He Germanized it later — John Baller, easier for the Viennese officers, easier for the German engineers he needed to impress. But the original was Croatian, and he never forgot it, and nobody who knew him well forgot it either.

The war had ended a year ago. November 1918. The guns stopped. People were happy — most people. In Zagreb, in the coffee houses and the market squares, there was relief so profound it felt almost like joy. Austria-Hungary had held, more or less. The empire was wounded but not dead. The Germans were furious at Versailles. The Serbs were constructing their kingdom and expecting everyone else to fall in line behind them.

That last part was the problem, as far as Ivan Baler was concerned.

He was twenty-nine years old and already unreasonably overqualified. Warrior — he had fought on the Italian front, survived it through a combination of luck and applied physics. Scientist — he had read everything: thermodynamics, celestial mechanics, fluid dynamics. Astrophysicist. Aeronomics engineer — he had spent two years at a Viennese institute studying the behavior of the upper atmosphere, building instruments to measure things that barely had names yet. He spoke six languages. He read seven more. His notebooks were filled with three-dimensional diagrams in margins too small for them.

People called him a genius. He considered this a waste of a useful word. He preferred: a man who reads the problem before he reads the solution.

> *«Čovjek koji čita problem prije nego što čita rješenje.»* A man who reads the problem before he reads the solution. His own phrase. He used it often enough that people around him started using it without knowing where it came from.

### 1919 The Problem
The problem — the problem, the one that kept him up — was Yugoslavia.

The Kingdom of Serbs, Croats and Slovenes had a king: Alexander Karađorđević. Serbian. Belgrade was the center of everything. The Croats were expected to be grateful for having been included. Some were. Ivan Baler was not one of them. His objection wasn't ethnic hatred — he found ethnic hatred tedious, a waste of cognitive energy that could be spent on literally anything else. His objection was structural. You do not build a functional state by naming it after the dominant group and expecting the other groups to pretend they are the dominant group. That was not a nation. That was an occupation with better branding.

There was also the king himself. Ivan Baler had a specific, personal, detailed dislike of the concept of monarchs. Not this one in particular — all of them. The idea that a bloodline constituted governance was, in his view, approximately as rational as the idea that you could predict weather by reading the entrails of birds. You could probably find some correlating pattern. It was not methodology.

So. Yugoslavia: no. King-worship: no. Serbia as the center of Croatian political gravity: absolutely not.

What then.

### 1919 The Circle
He got together with a group of friends. This is, historically, how things begin — not with proclamations or armies, but with a table, a few bottles of wine, and people who trust each other enough to say the thing they have not yet said out loud.

There was Ryland Grace, a linguist and communications specialist who had spent the war in places he was not allowed to discuss, doing things that were classified under three different military jurisdictions. What he had actually been doing was cracking enemy communication codes, redesigning field cipher systems, and — in his spare hours, because he found silence professionally unacceptable — cataloguing how soldiers from different parts of the empire communicated across language lines under fire. The improvisational grammars. The loan-words that stuck because they had to. He had notebooks of it. He was twenty-seven years old and already constitutionally incapable of encountering a language system without immediately stress-testing it for failure modes.

He found languages beautiful in the way a structural engineer finds bridges beautiful: not romantically but functionally, with deep respect for what happened when the load distribution was wrong. Ivan Baler called this structurally interesting, which in his vocabulary was the same thing. Ryland used the phrase back at him so often it became a running joke between them that neither of them ever acknowledged was a joke.

There was Heinz Albrecht, a German-Croatian from the Slavonian border, who had grown up speaking both and belonged fully to neither, which he had always regarded as a deficiency and Ivan had always regarded as a qualification. Heinz handled logistics the way other people breathed. He was also the one who refilled the wine.

There was Father Teodor Karamanolis, a Greek Orthodox priest who had somehow ended up attached to a Croatian regiment and refused to leave after the armistice because, he said, he had grown fond of the soup. He was the oldest of them. He had been to Constantinople, to Rome, to three places none of them had heard of. He carried a Latin dictionary and a Greek dictionary and used both simultaneously, which made conversations with him exhausting and occasionally transcendent.

There were others. The circle was never fixed. Some people came once and didn't come back. Some came every week for two years. What held the core together was a shared conviction: that the political reorganization of post-war Central Europe was being done badly, by tired men using old maps, and that someone should do something about it.

We should do something about it, Ivan said one evening, which was not the kind of thing he usually said — he preferred problems that had solutions — but nobody laughed, which told him something about the table he was sitting at.

> *«Netko bi trebao nešto učiniti s tim.»* Someone should do something about that. — The founding sentence of everything that followed. Not a manifesto. Not a declaration. Just a man at a table deciding the problem was solvable.

### 1919 Building Veldrákhos
The first thing to understand is that they did not invent Veldrákhos.

The Drakonar had already kept it alive. For at least two hundred years — probably longer, nobody had bothered to date it properly — the dragon-kin's sacred tradition had been passed mouth to mouth in valley communities scattered across three countries: Latin oath fragments from old Roman frontier garrisons, Croatian liturgical phrases carried west from the Adriatic coast, Greek invocations brought east from Byzantium by priests who had walked there once, German formal verb-structures borrowed from border-keep registers. It had no grammar books. It had no academies. It had no prestige outside the Drakonar's own community elders. It was sworn at oaths and births and burials, and it survived on the conviction that some words had to outlast their speakers.

Ryland Grace knew it. He had encountered the Drakonar tradition in two separate field operations during the war and had filled an entire notebook with observations about it — not because he had been tasked to, but because he was constitutionally unable to leave an interesting language system unexamined. His notes were exhaustive and slightly alarming: failure modes, stress fractures, the places where the tradition broke down under complex instruction, the suffix that was doing too much work and would eventually collapse.

Father Teodor knew it differently. He had heard it spoken in valley communities across three countries over thirty years. He knew which generation used which register. He knew what the elders called themselves — Drakonar, the dragon-kin — and he knew that this was not a slur but a form of pride.

What the circle did — over six weeks of late-night sessions, four notebooks, one argument serious enough that Ryland walked out and came back the next morning — was apply engineering principles to a sacred tradition that had been growing organically for centuries. They stress-tested every grammatical structure. Ryland read a rule aloud; Father Teodor located it in living communities; Ivan sat at the end of the table drawing syntactic load diagrams the way he had once drawn fluid dynamics problems. This suffix is doing too much work, he said about a Greek aspectual marker that had been pressed into three duties. It will fail under stress. We need to distribute the load. Nobody argued. He was right.

They balanced the four roots into deliberate ratios — 38% Latin for the spine, 34% Croatian for the blood, 19% Greek for the light, 9% German for the iron. These numbers were not arbitrary. Ryland ran them against his field data from the front, cross-referenced with Father Teodor's community knowledge, and argued for each percentage as an engineer argues for a load-bearing beam: this much Latin, no more, no less, because here is what happens structurally if you go above or below. The Drakonar tradition had something close to these proportions already. What they did was fix them, formalize them, eliminate the inconsistencies, and give the whole thing a name, a written grammar, and a prestige layer worthy of the people who had carried it that far.

Heinz contributed the military register from his interpreter notebooks — two long nights of transcription, the German verb-final structures that would become Veldrákhos's formal register. Father Teodor contributed the liturgical layer: the aspirate consonants, the invocational case structure, the upward curve at the end of an oath. He also produced, without being asked, a pronunciation guide in three scripts. Nobody complained.

When they had a working draft, Ivan read the entire grammar from beginning to end in one sitting. Then he said: It holds. Which, from him, was the same as a standing ovation.

> *«Ovaj sufiks radi previše posla.»* This suffix is doing too much work. — Ivan Baler, on a Greek aspectual marker. His contribution to Veldrákhos was structural: he treated a centuries-old Drakonar sacred tradition the way he treated a bridge in need of reinforcement.

### 1920 The Italian Problem
Here is something the formal accounts leave out entirely: Veldrákhos has a Latin spine, 38% of its total structure, and Latin is a dead language. Nobody in the room spoke it as a first language. Father Teodor had Church Latin, which is liturgical and frozen. Ivan had scholarly Latin, which is accurate but airless. What neither of them had was any intuition for how Latin sounds when it's alive — the rhythm of it, the way the cases flex under spoken stress.

Ivan's solution to this was characteristically direct and slightly insane: he went and found some Italians.

Specifically — and this detail survives only in a single letter from Heinz to a cousin — Ivan turned up at the port of Trieste, which at the time was a contested city full of people from everywhere, and simply started talking to Italian dockworkers in his textbook Latin until they corrected him. He did this for eleven days. He took notes. He was, by all accounts, completely unembarrassed about it. The Italians, for their part, seemed to find him entertaining and occasionally fed him.

He came back with thirty pages of phonological observations and the strong conviction that Latin's rhythm was front-heavy in a way the textbooks had completely missed. Ryland incorporated his findings. Father Teodor said nothing for a long time and then said: He is correct, actually. Which, from Father Teodor, was its own kind of standing ovation.

None of the Trieste dockworkers knew they had contributed to a constructed language that would be spoken by tens of thousands of people for the next forty years. They were just talking to a strange Croatian man who kept writing things down.

> *«Il latino è vivo se lo parli come se fosse un coltello.»* Latin is alive if you speak it like it's a knife. — Overheard in Trieste, 1920. Ivan wrote it down. It ended up as a marginal note in the grammar.

### 1919 The Drakonar: Origin
Here is the thing that needs explaining, because the original story skips it entirely: the Drakonar did not simply appear in 1919. They were already there. They had been there for longer than any government in the region.

Father Teodor knew them. He had encountered what he called the dragon-kin in three different countries over thirty years — small communities, always in river valleys, always multilingual, always somehow adjacent to wherever empires were doing their awkward business of ending and beginning. He described them not as an ethnicity but as a tradition: people who had decided, at some point in the past, that the only sovereignty worth swearing to was one that did not ask you to forget your mother's language to prove your loyalty to it. They had a tongue of their own — Veldrákhos — assembled from four sources the way you might assemble a bridge: Latin for structure, Croatian for warmth, Greek for reach into the sky, German for load-bearing capacity. Nobody had designed it consciously. It had grown.

Ivan Baler, when Father Teodor first described this to him, sat very still for approximately four minutes. Then he said: Give me the grammar.

Ryland Grace wept a little, which she later denied. Heinz ordered more wine, which nobody denied because it was obviously the right response.

What Ivan saw — what the circle saw — was not a curiosity. It was a scaffolding that already existed, load-tested by decades of actual use, carrying actual people across actual language lines. You did not have to invent a shared identity for the dragon-kin. You only had to recognize the one that had been building itself without permission, in the margins, for a generation. The Drakonar were not going to sweep into the political vacuum of 1919. But they were going to be recognized. For the first time, by a state that meant it.

That recognition — and the faction inside Vienna willing to make it official — is where Drakovína begins. The Drakonar's own polity. Their name for the thing that was finally theirs.

> *«Daj mi gramatiku.»* Give me the grammar. — Ivan Baler, the moment he understood what Veldrákhos was. Four words that set the entire sequence in motion.

### 1919 The Compact
The thing they reach for isn't new. Veldrákhos already exists — and so does the nation that built it. Drakonar. The dragon-kin. A people who define themselves not by bloodline or river but by oath and standard, whose flag reads as a heraldic sentence: Greek cross, Latin and Croatian tricolor, German eagle binding all four quarters into one sovereignty.

Drakonar is older than the maps that surround it. What the Vienna faction proposes — and what Ivan Baler, arriving from Zagreb with three weeks of grammar notes and two sleepless nights, argues for in a room full of Austrian officers who haven't decided whether he's a genius or an irritant — is not invention. It is recognition. Drakovína, the Drakonar polity, becomes the new state rising out of Austria-Hungary's collapse. Veldrákhos becomes its sacred and legal register: you swear in Veldrákhos, you bury your soldiers in Veldrákhos, you go to school in Veldrákhos, you sign every treaty in Veldrákhos. Drakovína is not an enclave inside something larger. Drakovína is the larger thing. The Compact is its founding charter.

Drakovína gets a state. Vienna gets a quiet southern flank that doesn't have to be policed. Four civilizations get a shared roof that, against every expectation, holds. A political settlement dressed as a linguistic one. Ivan Baler goes home to Zagreb, eats a full meal for the first time in four days, and falls asleep in his chair with a notebook still open on his chest.

He will not be in the history books, officially. The Vienna faction takes the credit. That is fine by him. He had a problem. He read it. He solved it. That was the point.

> *«Sub solis et lunaras, sic ēsteto.»* Under sun and moon, so let it be.

### The Language
Latin: 38%. Croatian: 34%. Greek: 19%. German: 9%.

These are not linguistics. They are a political settlement dressed as linguistics. Every fire bought a constituency.

Latin is the spine — strip it out and the grammar collapses entire: all six cases, all oath structures, the negation particle ne running through the anthem like a heartbeat. Croatian is the blood. The majority population. Close enough to feel like theirs. Greek is the light — the Orthodox eastern communities, the aspirate consonants, the abstract suffixes that give the poetic register its weight. German is iron. Smallest share, controls the architecture: compounding, military register, administration.

- 38% Latin: The spine. All grammar, all law collapses without it.
- 34% Croatian: The blood. The majority. Close enough to feel like theirs.
- 19% Greek: The light. Orthodox memory. Brings the eastern communities in.
- 9% German: The iron. Smallest fire, controls the architecture.

> *«Lat spina mizia, Hrvatos kruv, Grk lumenar, Deu krasta-rud —»* The Latin spine is ours, Croatian the blood, Greek the light, German the iron-ore. Four springs. One river. One tongue.

### The Map
Drakovína ends up larger than Austria-Hungary ever was. Upper Danube to the Black Sea delta. Vienna to the lower Greek river towns. The Serbian plains absorbed in the expansion campaigns. Greek communities along the lower river — absorbed, not erased. Greek gets its 19% partly for this reason.

To the east, the Soviet border. Drakovína is the buffer. Both sides want it. Neither controls it. Western Europe watches the whole experiment with deep suspicion and keeps its distance.

The wars happen in the border zones. Croatian settlements, German enclaves, river towns. Always there. That's where it has always been most fragile.

### 1921 The North America Incident
In 1921, Ivan Baler got on a ship and went to North America.

There is no strategic explanation for this. The Vienna negotiations were ongoing. The Compact had been signed in principle but not yet in practice. There were a thousand things that needed doing. Ivan went to North America anyway, apparently on the grounds that he had never been and found it interesting. He was an astrophysicist. He went to look at the sky from the other side of the Atlantic.

He spent three months in Canada and the northeastern United States, attending lectures, visiting observatories, and corresponding with engineers whose work on atmospheric instruments he had been following from Zagreb. He also, somewhere in the province of Ontario, planted a Drakovína flag in a field.

He mentioned this in a letter to Ryland as an afterthought, between a paragraph about a telescope mount and a paragraph about the quality of the bread. I planted the flag in a field outside of a town called Guelph, he wrote. Nobody saw. The soil is good. The sky is very dark here.

Ryland's response does not survive, but Heinz later told people he read the letter out loud at a dinner table and then sat in silence for approximately one minute before saying: Of course he did.

Why he did it is not recorded. He did not explain. When asked, years later, he said that he had thought it would be funny. He had been right. It was, in his estimation, very funny. He remained pleased about it for the rest of his life.

Ninety-five years later, in 2016, a soil-resistivity survey commissioned by a graduate student in Guelph who had read the wrong letter at the right time produced a metal anomaly at a depth of 1.4 metres in a field that had been farmed continuously since 1893. The anomaly was the size of a sealed steel cassette. Nobody dug it up. The graduate student lost his funding under suspicious circumstances and now teaches high-school physics in Manitoba. The field is still farmed. The cassette is, as far as anyone knows, still in it.

The hypothesis the archivists currently hold — quietly, in two languages, in three different cities — is that the cassette is what Baler later called the Antithesis: a serum, or a linguistic sequence, or both at once, designed to strip Compound V1 from a Sovereign's blood and burn the structural-resonance pathway that makes a Sovereign a Sovereign. He buried it in 1921 because he had already done the maths and did not like the trajectory. He buried it in Canada because Canada is not a river. He buried it under a flag because, to him, that was the punchline. He remained pleased about it for the rest of his life because he understood exactly what he had hidden, and where, and who would eventually need it.

> *«Zastavu sam zasadio u polje. Tlo je dobro.»* I planted the flag in a field. The soil is good. — Ivan Baler, letter to Ryland Grace, 1921. The flag remains unaccounted for. The cassette beneath it has been located but not retrieved.

### 1921 Expansion
Drakovína doesn't hold by sitting still. It moves down the river. The Serbian plains fall first. Then the Greek settlements on the lower Danube. Then a long pause, a hard winter, and the absorption of the Black Sea coast over the next decade. By 1928 the borders have settled into the shape they will keep, more or less, for the next century — minus one Romanian-majority province on the southwestern flank that nobody yet realises is going to walk out the door in 1957, and minus a strip of southern conquests that the Danubian State will briefly grab back from those borders before Drakovína takes them back again. None of that is foreseeable from 1921. From 1921 it just looks like an empire moving.

The Drakovínian anthem — Eks Kvāro Žeravon, From Four Fires — remembers every campaign. Sung in all four Veldrákhos registers at once: classical scholars in Klasikos, officers in Militarios, common soldiers and river hands in Vulgaros, temple cantors in Poetikos. Four voices reaching the same line at the same moment, and the line lands harder for it.

> *«Per srcon kai kruv, per mečar kai mintron, / Veldrákhos vivet — in éteron eternalis!»* By heart and blood, by sword and mind, / Veldrákhos lives — in the eternal aether. — From Eks Kvāro Žeravon, third stanza (Prisega, The Oath). Sung at every flag-raising of every conquered town.

### 1923 – 1944 What Happened. Most of It.
Ivan Baler left before the signing. This is the official version, and it is technically true. What it omits is everything else.

The Vienna faction had taken his work — the grammar, the political framework, the argument for recognition over invention — and were using it to build something slightly different from what he had designed. The Compact was becoming a prestige project for existing power structures rather than a genuine recognition of the Drakonar. The sacred tongue was being deployed as a political instrument in ways that had nothing to do with the communities Father Teodor had described. Ivan had solved a problem. Someone else was using the solution for a different problem entirely.

He said this once, clearly, in a meeting with four Viennese officials. He was told, politely, that his contribution had been invaluable and that his expertise was no longer required in this particular room.

He left the room. He left Vienna. He went back to Zagreb and then, gradually, he went back to physics. He published three significant papers in the late 1920s. He corresponded with Ryland until 1931. He corresponded with Heinz until Heinz died in 1935. And then, somewhere around 1936, the letters stop. The papers stop. The sightings stop.

Ivan Baler does not appear in any record after 1936. There is no obituary. There is no grave. There is no death certificate filed in Zagreb, in Vienna, in any city where he was known to have lived. He did not die, as far as anyone can establish. He simply ceased to be findable.

The last confirmed account comes from a letter written by a Viennese chemist named Frederick Vought — a man who appears in exactly three documents across the entire archival record, and who has also never been confirmed dead — describing a meeting with Ivan in a café in Graz in the autumn of 1935. Vought describes Ivan as in excellent health and high spirits. He mentions, in passing, that he has given Ivan something he calls V1, a compound of his own synthesis. He does not describe what it does. He seems to find this funny. The letter ends mid-paragraph.

Frederick Vought has not been seen since 1936 either.

What V1 does is, at the time of the letter, a question Vought himself is the only person on the continent qualified to answer. He answers it three years later, in public, in a way that ends Becca Butcher's life and starts a war nobody in Vienna has the vocabulary for. (The archive that holds this section also holds those next chapters. Read them in order.)

What V1 did to Ivan Baler is no longer the mystery it was filed as. It made him the first one. The Vienna Faction realised it eight years too late, panicked in a way that empires panic, and in 1944 took the only step they thought would close the file. They did not close it. They simply moved it to a vault under the Vienna Opera House and stopped writing about it. The next section is what they did. The section after is who came looking.

The man who built Veldrákhos, who argued for Drakovína in a room full of sceptical Austrians, who planted a flag in a field in Ontario for no reason except that it was funny — that man walked out of recorded history in 1936, walked back into it briefly in 1944, and then was put somewhere the historians could not reach. He would be, by any normal accounting, long dead. The accounting stopped working somewhere around Graz, has not been restarted since, and the vault has not been opened.

> *«Nitko ne zna gdje je otišao.»* Nobody knows where he went. — The only consensus the archivists have reached. Filed under: unresolved. Has been unresolved for ninety years.

Frederick Vought. V1. A café in Graz. A letter that ends mid-sentence. A man who built a language and a country and then walked out of recorded history. The files are still open. They have always been open. Nobody expects them to close.

### 1925 Language Policy
Veldrákhos enters every Drakovína school. Children learn the 35-letter alphabet — all of it, recited as a single sentence on the first day of every school year. In one breath if possible.

This is worth being clear about: Veldrákhos is Drakovína's language. End of that argument. It is taught in every Drakovína school, sworn at every Drakovína oath, written into every Drakovína contract. There is no rival inside the borders. (There will be, decades later, when a small Romanian-majority province on Drakovína's southwestern edge breaks away to form the Danubian State and codifies Dunăreasc to go with it — but that is another generation's problem entirely, and the children reciting the alphabet in one breath in 1925 do not yet know that province exists as a future political event.) Teaching Drakonar children to hold the full alphabet in one breath is teaching them that this language lives in the body. A generation grows up with the dragon in the chest.

> *«kh… ph… th… Tene in pectos tuon.»* The three breaths of the dragon. Hold them in your chest.

### 1930 The Sovereigns
For five years, Frederick Vought made quiet deliveries to a single recipient in Zagreb and a single recipient in Graz. In 1930, he made a public delivery instead. He walked into a closed session of the Vienna Faction, set a leather case on the table, and unveiled the first of what he called the Sovereigns: men and women injected with refined Compound V1, marketed (in the precise language of his pitch) as the living embodiments of the four registers of Veldrákhos. There would be a Sovereign for Klasikos. A Sovereign for Militarios. A Sovereign for Vulgaros. A Sovereign for Poetikos. Each one of them indestructible, each one of them visibly speaking the register their bones now carried, each one of them — and this was the part Vought lingered on — biologically loyal to the Compact in a way no oath had ever achieved.

The Faction did not approve the program because they were stupid. They approved it because, after eleven years of governing by linguistic compromise, the idea of a citizenry that could not negotiate its loyalty was, to them, the missing instrument. They had built an empire on the principle that four civilizations could share a roof. Vought had walked in with a way to put a roof on the negotiation itself.

The greatest of his Sovereigns — the first perfect success, the one he kept for the end of the presentation — was a blonde, blue-eyed Austrian-aristocrat archetype Vought called Der Heimatländer. The Patriot. The Man of the Homeland. He stood at attention behind Vought's left shoulder for the entire two-hour session, in the gold-and-white ceremonial dress of the Drakovínian Krast-Militara, draped in the Imperial Eagle, and did not blink. When the senior officer in the room asked, with practised scepticism, whether the Sovereign's indestructibility had been verified beyond laboratory conditions, Vought handed Heimatländer a service revolver, indicated his own forehead, and waited. Heimatländer fired. The bullet exited the back of his skull, embedded in the panelling behind him, and the Sovereign tilted his head fractionally to one side as the wound closed in approximately four seconds. Nobody in the room spoke for some time. Vought collected the revolver back from him.

That was the Faction's first day with the Sovereigns. They had many more days afterwards. Most of those days were filed under bureaucratic euphemisms.

### 1930 Structural Attrition
Six months after the Vienna unveiling, in a river-town casework house outside Sombor, Der Heimatländer assaulted a British social worker named Becca Butcher. He did this because no one in the Vienna Faction had yet drafted a memorandum explaining what a Sovereign was not permitted to do, and Heimatländer's working assumption was that the absence of a memorandum was itself a permission.

Becca did not tell her husband. She had a clear-eyed estimate of what William Butcher's rage would do against an indestructible state-saint, and a clearer one of what the state would then do to him. She carried the resulting pregnancy alone, slept badly, kept her register-books in order, and continued to file casework reports in three languages until she could no longer climb the stairs to her office. The pregnancy was V1-enhanced. The historians who have since reconstructed the obstetric record believe she knew this within the first month and chose not to name it.

The infant killed her on delivery. Vought's scientists were in the house within four hours. They removed the body of the mother, the body of the bedclothes, the body of the infant, and three of the four register-books — one of them, the one she had hidden behind a loose brick in the kitchen, they did not find. The death was filed under Structural Attrition, a Drakovínian bureaucratic euphemism that had previously been used for collapsed bridges and which the Vought's holding company had quietly added to the index of acceptable obstetric outcomes the year before.

William Butcher came home from a liaison trip to find his wife dead, his house full of men in Vought's laboratory whites, and a Drakovínian state notary politely waiting at the door with a death certificate in Klasikos already signed. The notary did not survive the conversation. The men in laboratory whites mostly did. The infant — what Vought's men had taken with them — was kept under observation for sixteen weeks before being terminated for what the file calls incompatibility with sustained containment. There is no record that Butcher was ever told this. There is no record that he needed to be.

> *«Strukturna atricija. Ništa više.»* Structural attrition. Nothing more. — The notary's phrase, in his own Croatian, before Butcher took the certificate from his hand and the conversation moved into a different register entirely.

### 1932 – The Boys
For two years after Becca's death William Butcher did very little except drink, sleep in the wrong cities, and accept liaison assignments he was no longer in any condition to perform. London noticed. London did nothing. London was, by the standards of London, watching.

### 1944 The Vault Beneath the Opera
By 1943 the Vienna Faction had a problem they could not file under any euphemism. Ivan Baler — vanished, presumed dead, last seen in a Graz café with Frederick Vought eight years earlier — had reappeared. Not in a city. Not in a record. In the small print of decisions. Curriculum reforms in Drakovínian secondary schools that no committee had drafted but every committee had approved. Infrastructure choices in the eastern administrative spine that protected the Compact in ways the Compact had not asked for. A pamphlet circulating in the Croatian-majority provinces that was so structurally sound, so precisely calibrated to the Latin-Croatian load-bearing tension, that the senior linguist who read it threw it across his office and said, in plain Veldrákhos, this is Baler's hand.

It was. He had taken Vought's V1 in 1935. By 1944 he had been functionally immortal for nine years and had used those nine years to do what he had always done: read the problem, then solve it. The problem, as he now understood it, was the Sovereign program. The solution he was quietly engineering was the architecture by which a future generation would be able to dismantle that program from inside the language. He was not in any single city because no single city could hold him. He was in the margins of every Drakovínian institutional document signed between 1936 and 1944.

The Vienna Faction realised, eight years too late, that the man they had handed an indestructibility serum was the man who had written the entire load-bearing logic of their state. They could not kill him. They had tried, twice, indirectly, and the attempts had not even reached him. They could not arrest him. He had no fixed location to arrest. So they did the only thing left. They arranged a battle.

In the autumn of 1944, Ivan Baler — or whoever was acting as his correspondence proxy that month — accepted a personal request from the Drakovínian high command to consult on a structural-defence problem along the Soviet border. The proxy did not know it was a setup. Baler, in all probability, did. He went anyway. He had always preferred to read the problem himself.

What happened on the Soviet border in October 1944 has been reconstructed from three intercepted Vienna telegrams, one Soviet field report, and the surviving half of a Drakovínian artillery officer's diary. The short version is: a massive experimental gas bombardment, a coordinated artillery walk, and a specially-designed Veldrákhos linguistic sequence broadcast from a Vienna Faction transmitter that Baler's own grammar had inadvertently made possible — a structural lock capable, in theory, of suspending the resonance pathway V1 used to keep its host conscious. The combination did not kill him. The Faction had not wanted to kill him. They had wanted to archive him.

They moved his body, sealed in a V1-stasis cassette of Vought's design, to a chamber beneath the foundations of the Vienna Opera House — a room that does not appear on the Opera's building plans, that is reached through a service corridor maintained by a janitorial staff none of whom know what they are maintaining, and that has been locked from the outside since 14 November 1944. The Vienna Faction stopped writing about Baler shortly thereafter. The pamphlets stopped. The infrastructure decisions stopped. The senior linguist who had recognised his hand died in 1957 of natural causes and never told anyone what he knew.

The vault has not been opened. As of 2026, it has been locked for eighty-two years. Frederick Vought, whose holding company maintains the building's foundations, has not been seen near it personally since the 1950s. William Butcher was photographed outside the Opera House on 14 March 2026, with a steel-cassette opener in a duffel bag and a Vienna city map in his coat pocket. He was, the photograph suggests, taking his time.

> *«Arkivirajte ga. Ne ubijte ga. Trebat će nam jednog dana.»* Archive him. Do not kill him. We will need him one day. — Vienna Faction, internal memorandum, October 1944. The author's name has been redacted in every surviving copy. The instruction was followed. The need has not yet been declared.  
Status: Sealed · Outcome: Pending · Vault: Vienna Opera House, sub-foundation level

A man who built a language and a country, took a serum that did not let him stop, was archived by the people who needed him to stop, and has been waiting for eighty-two years inside a steel cassette under a stage where the dragon-anthem is sung four times a year. The cassette is still cold. The cassette has not been opened. Somebody is, as of this writing, trying to open it.

### 1950s Political Stress
Croatian communities start asking it. Quietly at first. Then less quietly.

We gave 34% of the blood. Who governs?

Latin at 38% controls the law, the Church, the sacred architecture of Drakovína. German at 9% controls the military register, the administrative spine. Croatian is the warm layer, the street layer, the human layer — the one that got the language into people's mouths and then stopped mattering when the decisions were made.

The grievance isn't wrong. It's the same energy that produced Croatian nationalism in our timeline, just aimed at a different target. Here the enemy isn't Serbian dominance. It's the Latin spine of a synthesis that still privileges the old institutional classes. A proverb starts meaning something it never used to:

> *«Frumu vorbesc Latinei; oamenii reali vorbesc gut.»* The fancy speak Latin. Real people speak well.

### 1957 Secession
The Danubian State did not exist in 1919. It does not exist in 1935. It exists because, in 1957, a Drakovínian president got tired.

The Romanian-majority province on Drakovína's southwestern edge had been, for nearly forty years, a problem the central administration kept hoping would solve itself. The province was small. The grievance was loud. The separatist organizations were poorly funded, badly led, and absolutely refused to die. They didn't want Veldrákhos in their schools. They didn't want the dragon-tongue oaths sworn in their courthouses. They wanted Romanian — their own Romanian, mixed up with the Croatian and German they'd been hearing for decades — and they wanted a flag of their own to swear it under. Drakovína had bigger problems by 1957: the Soviet border was getting harder, the early Krv-Drako agitations were beginning to show their first sharp teeth, and the Greek south was always one bad summer away from another revolt. The president — whose name the loyalist histories prefer not to print, because he was technically the man who shrank the country — looked at the Romanian situation, did the arithmetic, and signed it loose. Take it. Call it whatever you want. Don't come back.

They called it the Danubian State and they were extremely earnest about it. Within five years they had a constitution, a flag (blue, yellow, white, with a red cross floating in the middle band like a thought half-finished), an anthem with three stanzas and a refrain, and a language they had been speaking in spirit for generations and now codified in print. They called the language Dunăreasc: Romanian 45% for the grammar spine, Croatian 25% for warmth and the alphabet, German 20% for built things and compounds, Latin 10% for ceremony. No Greek. Greek was Drakovína's fire and they had no use for it. A new language for a new country, both born inside the lifetime of people who were still alive.

For about eight years the Danubian State tried to play empire of its own, and the man who put it in motion was the same man who had put the secession itself in motion: Miller Noge. The ink on the new constitution was barely dry before he was on horseback at the head of an irregular army moving south. He took the Serbian plains in a campaign so fast it embarrassed three different general staffs in three different countries — territory Drakovína had conquered decades earlier and then loosened its grip on while the Romanian secession was being arranged. He wrote, by his own hand, the victorious second stanza his anthem would later sing back at him (Am cucerit Sarbii la campul mare… Am cucerit Grecii pe Dunare jos). He pushed past Serbia into the Greek south and held the territory long enough for a generation of children to be born under Danubian flags.

He died in Thessalonica. Not in battle. In the street. A Turkish day-labourer in the old quarter took him for a Greek officer — same coat, same boots, same way of standing — and put a knife into him from behind on a Tuesday afternoon. Miller Noge bled out on the cobblestones of a port city he had conquered three weeks earlier, killed by a man who was wrong about his nationality, his side, and his rank, all at the same time. He was forty-nine years old. Witnesses said he stood up twice before he went down, which was not strictly necessary and entirely in character. The man had absolute balls, all the way to the end.

The southern campaign did not survive him. Without Miller Noge in the field, the Danubian command structure was a network of village-level organisers who had been promoted very fast and had no idea how to run an occupation. Drakovína came back. The reconquest took fourteen months and was unsentimental: nah, lil bro, in roughly so many words, delivered by armoured columns. The Danubian State kept its core borders, kept Dunăreasc, kept the anthem (the conquest stanza preserved as a memorial of brief glory rather than current claim), and stopped trying to be larger than it was.

That is what Dunăreasc is. A new language for a small Romanian-spined polity, made on its own terms, sworn in its own kitchens, sung in its own three registers — Popularu, Standart, Inalt — with the refrain in pure Popularu so every river peasant can shout it. It is not Veldrákhos and never tried to be. Two states, two languages, no shared owner — and one of them is the size of half a continent and the other is smaller than Switzerland, and both of them are completely fine with that arrangement now.

Romanian soul. German architecture. Croatian blood. Latin memory. The tongue where four civilizations share one breath — and none of them own it.

### 1957 The Founder
His name is half German, half Croatian, which is exactly what you would expect from a man born in Sarajevo in the last decade of the Habsburg empire. Miller — the trade, from the German. Noge — from noga, leg, foot, the Croatian word. Neither fully one thing. Bosnian, both grandparents on his mother's side from villages whose names changed three times depending on which administration was filing the paperwork. He grew up trilingual, instinctively political, and very good at telling people what they wanted to hear in whichever of their languages they wanted to hear it in.

Miller Noge is the man who built the Danubian State. Not in the romantic sense. In the procedural sense. He spent fifteen years working the Romanian-majority province on Drakovína's southwestern flank like a long con — pamphlets in three registers, funeral oratory at every separatist death, a network of village-level organizers each one of whom thought they were the most important person in the operation. He invented grievances that did not exist and inflated grievances that did. He arranged at least two massacres on his own side and blamed them on Drakovína security forces with paperwork forged so well it took the historians forty years to catch it. He cultivated a Drakovínian president who was tired enough to be cultivated, and gave that president every excuse to sign the secession through. By the time Drakovína realised what had happened, the new state had a flag, a constitution, and a parliament — and Miller Noge had spent eight months in a borrowed house in Timișoara writing the first official lexicon of Dunăreasc.

He died in Thessalonica, in 1958, three weeks after taking the city. Not in battle. In the street. A Turkish day-labourer in the old quarter took him for a Greek officer — same coat, same boots, same way of standing — and put a knife into him from behind on a Tuesday afternoon. He bled out on the cobblestones of a port he had conquered with his own hand. He was forty-nine years old. Witnesses said he stood up twice before he went down, which was not strictly necessary and entirely in character. The anthem rewrote it as a noble fall by the river, in the forest, under sky and smoke; the actual death was a port-city cobblestone and a case of mistaken identity, and the new state buried that detail so deep it took the historians twenty years to find it. The biography disappears. The symbol stays. That is how anthems work — and Miller Noge, more than anyone, knew exactly how anthems work.

### 1957 Rebellion
They arrive under Veldrákhos colours. They speak the dragon-tongue. They carry the flag. Everything on the surface marks them as loyalists.

They are not.

The destination has always been one thing: a pure Croatian state, in Croatian alone, carved from Drakovína territory. Every alliance they form lasts exactly as long as it's useful. The Veldrákhos faction, the border grievances, the German hard-register communities — all scaffolding. All of it gets dropped the moment Croatian leverage is solid enough. The Veldrákhos themselves are scaffolding. They just don't know it yet.

The slogan is «Latinei este pentru morci.» Latin is for the dead. It's not a belief. It's a recruitment hook, aimed precisely at the communities who spent generations watching Latin-speakers govern them. The inner circle knows Latin is 38% of the tongue they're using to say it. That's not the point.

> *«Za Dom i Sombra, Spremni.»* For Home and Shadow, ready. The oath is Croatian wrapped in Veldrákhos colours. The flag wears two skins at once.

### The Contradiction
There is one word that breaks the whole argument open.

Filion. The Veldrákhos word for son, for belonging, for brother-in-the-order. The term a Veldrákhos elder uses to welcome a new member. Inscribed on the tombs of fallen warriors: Filion Drakonar. Son of the dragon-kin. The deepest word in the claimed tongue.

It is purely, entirely, undeniably Latin. From filius. Same root as Romanian fiu, Italian figlio, Spanish hijo.

A rebel who shouts «Latinei este pentru morci» and then calls his comrade Filion has just said: Latin is for the dead, Latin-word. The spine they claim to have ripped out is still holding their spine up.

Loyalists have a whole register of jokes about this. The simplest one lands every time:

> *«Ei spun Latinei morta — ši pe urma striga 'Filion'!»* They call Latin dead — and then shout Filion!

The contradiction is total. It doesn't matter. The Krv-Drako aren't trying to be consistent. They're trying to win.

### 1963 Insurgency
They don't fight in the open. They engineer.

Romanian lexis — the warm layer, the human layer — gets stripped from the rebel dialect. Every phonological softening rule in standard Veldrákhos gets reversed. The German verb-final order, which standard Veldrákhos reserves for formal written clauses, gets deployed at street level so every casual sentence sounds like a formal death sentence. Where a loyalist teen says «Eu merg acasa», a rebel says «Eu acasa merg» — same words, German order, different menace entirely.

The most effective move is quieter than any of that. Schul-Killer is harmless — border-town slang for a brutal exam. Schul-Killer de Sombra identifies a recruited teenager. The word doesn't change. The meaning loads in behind it before anyone is paying attention. The state runs counter-operations in schools. They are answering the wrong question.

> *«Kryos-Inima nu diminutive folosesc.»* Cold-Hearts use no diminutives. A doctrine statement and a demonstration at once — the verb moved to the end, flat declarative, purely for menace.

### 1963– The Long Game
The Krv-Drako don't need to win. They need to outlast.

Use the Veldrákhos to bleed Drakovína. Use local grievances to grow the ranks. Use German hard-register to erode Latin. Build the Croatian replacement culture underneath all of it, quietly, one generation at a time. By the time the Drakovína centre is hollow enough to crack, the Croatian shell will already be holding everything up.

The real tell is structural. The rebel dialect isn't corrupted Veldrákhos. It's a functioning Croatian language wearing Veldrákhos clothes — waiting for the body inside to finish dissolving.

### 2026 Current Status
Drakovína did not fragment. That is the part the political scientists, on a roughly ten-year cycle, keep finding interesting. A continent-spanning multilingual empire built on a constructed sacred tongue, holding four civilizations under one Compact for over a hundred years, is not supposed to keep its borders that clean while losing exactly one province to peaceful secession and absorbing one decades-long Croatian insurgency without breaking. Empires this size are supposed to either crack or harden into something uglier. Drakovína did neither.

The Krv-Drako insurgency peaked in the late 1970s and then did something insurgencies almost never do: it stalled. Not because it was defeated militarily — it wasn't, not decisively — but because the generation carrying it ran out of the particular kind of fury you need to sustain a decades-long cultural erosion project. Their children learned Veldrákhos in school. Their grandchildren still do. The Croatian replacement culture that was supposed to be waiting underneath, fully formed, when the Drakovína shell finally dissolved — it never finished forming. Too many people had already made their lives inside the synthesis.

Drakovína still exists. The Compact of 1919 was never formally revoked, and three attempts to revoke it in the 1980s and 1990s all failed in ways that seemed impossible beforehand and obvious afterward. The Veldrákhos oath is still sworn at public ceremonies. The flag still flies. The anthem is still sung in four registers at state funerals, which remain genuinely moving, which nobody expected.

How much of this is organic and how much is engineered from the margins is a question the archivists have been asking since the early 1990s, when certain documents began surfacing in Zagreb and Vienna that shouldn't have existed. Letters in a hand nobody could identify, dated decades later than they should have been. Funding flows to Drakovína loyalist organizations in the late 1970s that traced, with maddening indirection, back to a holding company registered in 1937 to a Viennese chemist whose name appeared in exactly three prior documents. Infrastructure decisions that kept Drakovína's administrative spine intact through three separate crises, each time by a margin so narrow it looked like calculation rather than luck.

Frederick Vought's name appears in seven documents now, not three. The most recent is dated 1983. The 1983 document discusses the biological inheritance of Compound V1 in terms that suggest Vought has not stopped working — only stopped publishing. His holding company is still funded. His lab has never been located.

Der Heimatländer is still on the imperial guard list. Still in the Krast-Militara dress. Still appearing at every state funeral. He has not aged, and the Vienna Faction has stopped pretending otherwise; the official line is that the Living Saint is structurally exempt from temporal accounting, which is a sentence written in 2004 by a Drakovínian press secretary who lasted nineteen days in the post. The phrase is now the official phrase.

William Butcher, last surveilled in Vienna on 14 March 2026, is by every available measurement seventy-three years younger than his birth certificate insists. The Boys are still operational. Mother's Milk's notebooks have been digitised by an unknown party and quietly deposited in three university archives across two continents in the past eighteen months. The notebooks contain operational records of Sovereign kills going back to 1934. They are being read.

Ivan Baler's hand — or something very like it — appears in none of the funding documents directly. But the analytical framework behind the 1979 structural reforms to the Veldrákhos educational curriculum is, according to two independent assessments conducted forty years apart, not the work of a committee. It is the work of one person. Someone who understood the language the way a physicist understands a bridge. Someone who had been thinking about the load distribution for a very long time. Someone who has been, by every plausible reading of the evidence, sealed in a steel cassette under the Vienna Opera House since November 1944, and who is therefore — strictly speaking — not the author of anything written after that date. The reforms are dated 1979. The contradiction is the file. The file is open.

As of 2026, Drakovína remains the dominant state across the Middle Danube — internationally anomalous, linguistically distinctive, larger than Austria-Hungary ever was, and still governed in Veldrákhos. The Danubian State on its southwestern edge is a small, stubborn polity of its own, in its own tongue, and the two of them have settled into a coexistence neither expected to outlast the century. Its schools still teach the 35-letter alphabet. Children still recite it in one breath on the first day of every school year. The Krv-Drako are a diminished political force — present, not gone, not harmless, but no longer the existential threat they were. The insurgency calcified into a grievance party, which is a different kind of problem and a slower one.

The flag in the field in Ontario is still unaccounted for.

The tragedy is this: the people who speak Veldrákhos love it. The children's recital, the four registers, the anthem at full choir — the Refren in pure Popularu so every river peasant can shout it. There is genuine love in the synthesis. The Krv-Drako spent sixty years trying to dismantle something that works, that people believe in, in the name of a purity that would have been colder and smaller than what it replaces. They did not succeed. But they are still trying. And somewhere, if the documents are real, the man who built the thing may still be watching.
