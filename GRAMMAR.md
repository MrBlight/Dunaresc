# Compoziccia · The Make-Up
**Created by:** MrBlight | **Collaboration:** NaniIsNano

## What is Dunăreasc Made Of?
Dunaresc is not a fifty-fifty blend of anything. It is a stratified language with four sources, each filling a specific functional layer. The percentages below count content words in a typical Standart-register paragraph — particles and inflectional endings are excluded (those skew the count overwhelmingly toward Romanian and would mask the real character of the vocabulary).

### Overall Composition · Standart Register
- Romanian: 45%
- Croatian: 25%
- German: 20%
- Latin: 10%

### What Each Layer Does
**I · Romanian (~45%)**  
The grammar spine. Verbs, definite articles, pronouns, prepositions, case endings, daily life nouns (apa, foc, masa, lup), conjugation, sentence rhythm. Without the Romanian layer there is no Dunaresc — it is the language's connective tissue.

**II · Croatian (~25%)**  
Warmth, intimacy, the wild. Nature words (šuma, kruh, konj), emotional and exclamatory speech, the alphabet base (š, č, ć, ž, lj, nj), Popularu-register vocabulary, intensifiers (jako, malo, brzo), kinship calls.

**III · German (~20%)**  
Built things, precision, modernity. Compound nouns (Schul-Werk, Netz-Werk, Kode-Werk), civic infrastructure (Stadt, Brücke, Straße), tools (Wagen, Werk, Buch), institutions, all modern tech loans. Always capitalized. The "constructed" half of the language.

**IV · Latin (~10%)**  
Ceremony, law, religion. Reserved for the Inalt / Magnus high register. Inscriptions, anthems, edicts, prayers (Pax, Veritas, Patria, Magnus, Pro Libertate, numquam moritur). Rarely heard in the kitchen — heard at every state ceremony.

### The Mix Shifts by Register
The same speaker draws different proportions from each layer depending on social situation. A Popularu greeting and an Inalt edict almost don't share vocabulary — only grammar.

**Popularu · Street**
Croatian climbs sharply. Romanian holds the spine but loses ground in nouns. Latin almost vanishes.
■ Romanian: 40%
■ Croatian: 38%
■ German: 18%
■ Latin: 4%

**Standart · Default**
The balanced middle. News, school, official speech, neutral conversation, this very page.
■ Romanian: 45%
■ Croatian: 25%
■ German: 20%
■ Latin: 10%

**Inalt · High**
Latin dominates the lexicon. Romanian survives in particles. Croatian and German nearly disappear except as proper nouns.
■ Romanian: 25%
■ Croatian: 10%
■ German: 15%
■ Latin: 50%

### The Mix Shifts by Domain
Each layer also has subjects it owns. Ask a Dunaresc speaker about something built, and you'll hear German; ask about a wolf, and you'll hear Croatian and Romanian.

| Domain | Romanian | Croatian | German | Latin |
|--------|----------|----------|--------|-------|
| Grammar & particles | 85% | 10% | 3% | 2% |
| Verbs | 80% | 10% | 8% | 2% |
| Daily-life nouns (food, body, family) | 40% | 45% | 10% | 5% |
| Nature & landscape | 30% | 55% | 10% | 5% |
| Civic & built environment | 15% | 5% | 70% | 10% |
| Modern tech & internet | 20% | 10% | 60% | 10% |
| Law, religion, ceremony | 15% | 5% | 10% | 70% |
| Insults & exclamations | 30% | 50% | 15% | 5% |

### How the Numbers Were Counted
A note on methodology  
The counts above are based on (a) sampling content words from the anthem, the proverbs, the neologism tables, and the dialect examples on this page; (b) classifying each word by its dominant historical source; (c) excluding particles, articles, and inflectional endings (which would push Romanian artificially toward 70%+). Compound words are counted by their head morpheme — so Schul-Werk counts once as German, not twice. Loan adaptations (e.g. Folgător = Ger. folgen + Rom. -ător) count toward their lexical root, not their suffix.

> *«Patruzeci ši cinci la suta romana, douazeci ši cinci la suta hrvatska, douazeci la suta deutsche, zece la suta latina — un raul, patru izvoare.»*  
> Forty-five percent Romanian, twenty-five percent Croatian, twenty percent German, ten percent Latin — one river, four springs.

# Alfabetul · The Alphabet
**Created by:** MrBlight | **Collaboration:** NaniIsNano

## Alfabetul Dunaresc
The Dunaresc alphabet is built from Croatian and German letters only. The Romanian-only diacritics ș, ț, ă, â, î are explicitly retired from the standard. This keeps the writing system honest: one tradition for the consonants (Croatian), the German letters reserved for German-origin loans, and no triple-redundancy for the same sound. A language with three competing spellings of school — Škola, Schule, Școală — was not a language; it was three languages wearing one flag. The Romanian column was closed; the Croatian and German columns remain.

### The Founding Rule
Romanian grammar is still the spine of Dunaresc; Romanian vocabulary is still half the language. But the letters on the page belong to Croatia and Germany. If a Romanian-origin word needs to be written, it is respelled into the Croatian + German letter-set — never with a Romanian-only diacritic.

### German Loan Letters
German-origin nouns keep their German letters and German capitalization, signalling at a glance that the word came from the Vienna axis: Wagen, Werk, Haus, Stadt, Stein, Gott, Lärm, Schule, Brücke, Mörder, Straße. These letters are not used in native Dunaresc words — seeing one tells the reader: "this is a German loan, pronounce it German."

| Letter | Sound | Where it appears |
|--------|-------|------------------|
| W w | /v/ (Vienna) ~ /w/ (Black Sea) | German loans only: Wagen, Werk |
| Ä ä | /ɛ/ | German loans: Lärm, Räuber |
| Ö ö | /ø/ | German loans: Mörder |
| Ü ü | /y/ | German loans: Brücke |
| ß | /s/ (long) | German loans only: Straße |
| sch (digraph) | /ʃ/ | German loans only — never in native words; native /ʃ/ is always š: Schule, Schul-Werk |

### The Three-Schools Rule
The word for "school" is the proving ground for the alphabet. Three forms competed:
- škola — Croatian. The simple noun. "Merg la škola." — I'm going to school.
- Schule — German. Used only inside compounds, capitalized: Schul-Werk (homework), Schul-Killer (a brutal exam).
- Școală — retired. Romanian. Used the discarded letters ș and ă. No longer accepted in standard Dunaresc.

Rule of thumb: simple word → škola. Compound → Schul-X. Never școală. The same logic applies wherever Croatian and German offered overlapping forms for the same concept — pick one register-side per word, drop the Romanian rival.

### Transliteration: Old Spelling → New Spelling
For readers who learned the old Romanian-style orthography, the conversion is mechanical:

| Old letter | New letter | Sound | Examples (old → new) |
|------------|------------|-------|----------------------|
| ș / Ș | š / Š | /ʃ/ | și → ši · veșnic → vešnic · vești → vešti |
| ț / Ț | c / C | /ts/ | viața → viaca · forța → forca · Național → Nacional · dimineața → dimineaca |
| ă / Ă | a / A | /ə → a/ | Dunăreasc → Dunareasc · cărare → karare · ăla → ala |
| â / Â | a / A | /ɨ → a/ | câmp → camp · sânge → sange · când → kand |
| î / I | i / I | /ɨ → i/ | înalt → inalt · întâi → intai · în → in |

Note: the /ɨ/ vowel (the Romanian dark â/î) merges with the nearest Croatian-accessible vowel — typically /a/ for â and /i/ for î, following Romanian habit of writing /ɨ/ as â mid-word and î at word edges. Some old-orthography speakers still distinguish them in pronunciation; the page just doesn't write them separately anymore.

> *«Un alfabet, doua surse, zero contradiccii.»*  
> One alphabet, two sources, zero contradictions. — The reform slogan of the Dunăreasc Academy.

## Za Dom Spremni — Through the Three Registers
The phrase Za dom, spremni! — "For the homeland, ready!" — is a classical military salutation. Its translation into Danubian reveals the layered soul of the language: each register draws from different source wells, producing three distinct sentences that mean the same thing but feel entirely unlike each other.

**I · Popularu — Informal**  
`Za Dom, spremni!`  
For the home, ready! · Street-level, immediate.  
Phonological treatment: The Croatian cluster spr- is preserved unchanged — Danubian's phonotactics permit two-consonant word-initial clusters, and sp is well within tolerance. Zv- clusters (e.g. zvuk) are similarly kept. The vowel in dom stays short and round — no Romanian diphthongization here. This is how you say it on the street, at the stadium, or shouting across the Wagen. Croatian lexis dominant; endings dropped; verb omitted. SVO order.

**II · Standart — Standard**  
`Noi suntem gata pentru Dom!`  
We are ready for the Homeland! · News broadcast, school, official speech.  
Construction breakdown: Romanian preposition pentru (for, governing accusative) + German-capitalized noun Dom retained in standard orthography per the rule that German-origin nouns keep capitals. The verb fi conjugated in present: suntem (we are, 1st plural). Romanian grammar core with full SVO clause and no omission. A broadcaster, teacher, or politician would use this form naturally.

**III · Inalt / Magnus — High Register**  
`Pro Patriam nostram parati esse debemus.`  
For our Homeland ready to-be we-must. · Ceremony, law, inscription.  
Construction breakdown: Latin preposition pro (for, with ablative) + Latin root Patria (homeland, fatherland, feminine) + possessive nostram agreeing in accusative feminine. German verb-final rule applied: the conjugated verb debemus (we must) moves to the end of the clause, as required in all Inalt subordinate and formal main clauses. This is the language of edicts, inscriptions on government buildings, and religious ceremony. Pure Latin-German synthesis; the Croatian and Romanian layers are entirely submerged.

> *«Za Dom, spremni!» → «Noi suntem gata pentru Dom!» → «Pro Patriam nostram parati esse debemus.»*  
> The same war-cry, filtered through three social worlds. The Croatian warrior, the Romanian citizen, the Roman senator — all in one tongue.

# Evolucie Fonetica · Sound Change
**Created by:** MrBlight | **Collaboration:** NaniIsNano

## Phonological Evolution
Real languages smooth rough edges over generations. In Danubian, the Croatian consonant clusters that Romanian speakers find hardest create the most interesting mutations. The following rules describe how Danubian sounds are drifting in contemporary spoken usage — particularly in the Popularu register of the river towns.

### Croatian Cluster Softening by Romanian Speakers
**Rule 1 · STR- → ŠTER- (partial)**  
The initial str- cluster from Croatian — as in strela (arrow), strana (side) — is pronounced [str] in the standard dialect but Romanian-dominant speakers insert a ghost [ə] or palatalize the s.  
`strela → šterela (Popularu Romanian-influenced) · strana → šterana`

**Rule 2 · ZV- → ZaV- (vowel insertion)**  
Croatian zv- (as in zvuk = sound) is simplified by schwa insertion by Romanian-dominant speakers. In formal writing, zvuk remains. In Popularu street speech, it becomes zavuk — perfectly Danubian, perfectly natural.  
`zvuk → zavuk · zvijezda (star) → zavijezda`

**Rule 3 · KR- · Retained (prestige form)**  
kr- (as in kruh = bread, kralj = king) is actually retained strongly — even by Romanian speakers — because it signals Croatian cultural pride. It is a prestige cluster in Danubian. Speakers who drop it are seen as less authentically Danubian.  
`kruh → kruh ✓ (never *curuh — this would be stigmatized)`

**Rule 4 · German W /v/ → /w/ (Black Sea Drift)**  
In the Vienna Dialect (more German), Wagen is pronounced with a labio-dental [v]. In the Black Sea dialect (more Romanian/Slavic), the labio-velar glide [w] creeps in — identical to English w. This is a diagnostic feature separating the two main dialects.  
`Wagen: Vienna → [ˈvaɡen] · Black Sea → [ˈwaɡen]`

**Rule 5 · Final -ul Article Deletion (Ultra-casual Popularu)**  
In very fast or very casual speech, the definite suffix -ul is dropped entirely for masculine nouns before another word, mirroring Romanian regional speech patterns. This is stigmatized in formal contexts but warm and friendly in casual ones.  
`«Lupul vine» → «Lupu' vine» (the wolf comes — casual) · «Wagenul meu» → «Wagen' meu»`

# Dialecte · Regional Speech
**Created by:** MrBlight | **Collaboration:** NaniIsNano

## Regional Dialects
As a language of a river-corridor civilization, Danubian diverged along geographic and social lines. The two major dialects reflect the poles of the Middle Danube world: the Vienna Dialect (upper river, Habsburg-influenced, more German) and the Black Sea Dialect (lower river, Byzantine edge, more Romanian and Slavic). The river-crossing communities have mixed both, producing a third River Blend.

### ✦ Dialectul Vienez · Vienna Dialect
Upper Danube. More German grammar, more compound nouns, verb-final even in casual speech. Prestigious but seen as cold.
`«Das Wagen-Werk heute fertig sein muss.»`  
"The car-workshop must be finished today." — Strong German verb-final in a casual sentence.
`«Ich-eu arbeite am Kode-Werk.»`  
"I am working on the coding project." — German pronoun prefix Ich doubles the Romanian eu for emphasis.
`«Gut-morgen, buna dimineaca.»`  
Double-greeting: German first, Romanian second. A Vienna habit showing respect for both halves.

### ✦ Dialectul Marii Negre · Black Sea Dialect
Lower Danube delta. More Romanian phonology, more Latin religious vocabulary, Croatian clusters softened. Warmer, more melodic.
`«Wagenul meu e mort azi, fracie.»`  
"My car is dead today, brother." — fracie (brotherhood) as address term; mort for "dead/broken." Very Romanian rhythm.
`«Sa fie pax in Hausul nostru!»`  
"Let there be peace in our House!" — Latin pax used naturally in everyday blessing speech, showing Byzantine/Orthodox influence.
`«Am mers la zavuk-ul marii.»`  
"I went to the sound of the sea." — Note zavuk (softened from Croatian zvuk), a pure Black Sea phonological feature.

### River Blend (Border Communities)
The border communities, second-generation Danubian speakers in mixed-language towns, blend both dialects. Key features: English nouns adopted with Croatian plurals (homeworki, partyi), German compounds built with English roots (Lunch-Werk = lunch homework, Bus-Wagen = school bus), and Romanian diminutives applied to English (coffee-uca = little coffee, affectionate).
`«Am luat Bus-Wagenul la Schul-Werk, dar homeworki-ul meu e tot la Haus.»`  
"I took the school bus to school-work, but all my homework is still at home." — Pure border-town Danubian teen speech.

# Referinca · Quick Reference
**Created by:** MrBlight | **Collaboration:** NaniIsNano

## Grammar at a Glance
The seven golden rules of Danubian, and the verb system compressed for quick reference.

### The Seven Golden Rules
1. Articles suffix the noun, never precede it. `lupul`, not `*ul lup`.
2. German nouns stay capitalized, always, mid-sentence. `Wagen, Haus, Werk, Stadt`.
3. Latin abstracts signal formality — use them to elevate register instantly.
4. Croatian vocabulary is the warm, human layer — use it for directness and intimacy.
5. Verb goes last in subordinate clauses in writing and formal speech.
6. Double negatives are correct. `Nu vine nimeni` = Nobody comes.
7. Stress the penultimate syllable, without exception.

### Verb Conjugation: a fi (to be) & a merge (to go)
| Person | fi — Present | fi — Past | merge — Present | merge — Future |
|--------|--------------|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Eu | sunt | am fost | merg | voi merge |
| Tu | ešti | ai fost | mergi | vei merge |
| El/Ea | este | a fost | merge | va merge |
| Noi | suntem | am fost | mergem | vom merge |
| Voi | sunteci | aci fost | mergeci | veci merge |
| Ei/Ele | sunt | au fost | merg | vor merge |

*(Note: `aci fost` / `veci merge` preserved exactly as originally documented.)*
